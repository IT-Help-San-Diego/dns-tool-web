SPF allows domain owners to specify which mail servers are authorized to send email on behalf of their domain. Without SPF, any server can forge the envelope sender.

Every finding maps to a specific RFC and includes terminal commands for independent verification. No proprietary databases. No black boxes.

Three signals: (1) SPF record presence and mechanism — does the domain declare authorized senders? (2) DMARC policy level — p=none means monitoring only, no enforcement. p=quarantine sends suspicious mail to spam. p=reject instructs receivers to refuse unauthorized messages entirely. (3) DKIM key publication and strength — are signing keys published, and are they strong enough to resist brute-force? A domain with no DMARC or p=none is operationally spoofable — receivers will deliver forged messages to the inbox. The verdict badges in this report tell you the disposition at a glance.

Without DNSSEC, DNS responses are unsigned plaintext — vulnerable to cache poisoning, on-path manipulation, and BGP hijacking of resolver traffic. An unsigned domain means an attacker with network position can redirect traffic by injecting forged DNS responses. DNSSEC adds cryptographic signatures to every DNS response, creating a chain of trust from the root zone to the target domain. This report checks the AD (Authenticated Data) flag across multiple resolvers. No AD flag means the domain is operating without DNS integrity verification.

DNS records are public by design — the DNS protocol exists to serve records to anyone who asks. Every query this tool makes is a standard DNS lookup, HTTPS request, or SMTP banner connection that any mail server performs millions of times daily. No exploitation, no authentication bypass, no authorization required. This is passive OSINT collection — reading publicly observable data and producing intelligence from it.
